The Traveling Salesman Problem

Why delivering to 50 cities is one of the hardest problems in computer science — and how approximation algorithms save us.

📄 Rosenkrantz et al., 1977 · SIAM Journal on Computing ⏱ ~10 min read
01 — Problem Definition

What is TSP?

Given a set of n cities and the distances between every pair, find the shortest possible route that visits each city exactly once and returns to the origin.

Simple to state. Deceptively hard to solve.

📦
Delivery Routing
Amazon optimizes routes for thousands of packages daily. Even a 1% improvement saves millions.
🧬
DNA Sequencing
Gene sequencing problems map directly to TSP — minimizing the number of chemical reactions needed.
🔬
Circuit Board Drilling
Manufacturing PCBs: minimize drill head travel distance across thousands of holes.
02 — Complexity Theory

Why Exact Solutions Are Impossible (at Scale)

TSP belongs to a class of problems called NP-Complete. No polynomial-time algorithm is known — and most computer scientists believe none exists.

Cities (n) Permutations to Check Time @ 10⁹ ops/sec Verdict
10 181,440 0.18 ms ✓ Feasible
20 60,822,550,204,416,000 ~2 years ⚠ Very slow
50 3 × 10⁶² ∞ (longer than universe) ✗ Impossible
💡
P vs NP — The million-dollar question. If P = NP were proven, TSP (and hundreds of other problems in cryptography, logistics, and AI) could be solved efficiently. Almost all researchers believe P ≠ NP.
03 — Our Algorithms

Three Approaches, Three Trade-offs

Algorithm 1 — Nearest Neighbor (Greedy)

O(n²)

Strategy: Always go to the closest unvisited city.

Why it fails: The greedy choice is locally optimal but globally disastrous. By always picking the nearest city, the algorithm can "paint itself into a corner" — forcing a very long jump at the end to close the tour.

Pseudocode
tour ← [start_city]
unvisited ← all other cities

while unvisited is not empty:
    current ← last city in tour
    nearest ← city in unvisited with min dist(current, city)
    tour.append(nearest)
    unvisited.remove(nearest)

tour.append(start_city)  // close loop
No quality guarantee O(n²) time O(n) space

Algorithm 2 — MST 2-Approximation

O(n² log n)

Strategy: Build a Minimum Spanning Tree, then follow it with DFS.

The key theorem: For any metric TSP instance, the MST weight is a lower bound on the optimal tour. A DFS traversal of the MST visits every edge at most twice, giving a tour of cost ≤ 2 × MST ≤ 2 × OPT.

Proof of 2× Guarantee
① Let OPT = length of the optimal tour.
② Removing any edge from OPT gives a spanning tree. So: MST_cost ≤ OPT
③ A full DFS walk of the MST traverses each edge exactly twice: DFS_walk = 2 × MST_cost
④ By the triangle inequality (metric TSP), shortcutting repeated visits never increases cost.
∴ tour_cost ≤ 2 × MST_cost ≤ 2 × OPT  ■
Pseudocode
T ← Prim_MST(cities)         // O(n²)
tour ← DFS_preorder(T, root=0) // O(n)
tour.append(tour[0])           // close loop
≤ 2× optimal guaranteed O(n² log n)

Algorithm 3 — 2-opt Local Search

O(n² · k)

Strategy: Repeatedly find two edges that "cross" and uncross them.

Intuition: If edges (A→B) and (C→D) cross each other on the map, replacing them with (A→C) and (B→D) always shortens the tour (triangle inequality). Keep swapping until no improvement is possible.

Pseudocode
tour ← nearest_neighbor_tour()
improved ← true

while improved:
    improved ← false
    for i in range(1, n-1):
        for j in range(i+1, n):
            if dist(tour[i-1], tour[i]) + dist(tour[j], tour[j+1])
               > dist(tour[i-1], tour[j]) + dist(tour[i], tour[j+1]):
                  reverse(tour[i..j])    // uncross edges
                  improved ← true
Local optimum (no global guarantee) O(n² · iterations) Best practical results
